Adam Rippon dominates 'DWTS,' Mirai Nagasu goes home, Tonya Harding makes the finals May 14, 2018 Updated May 15, 2018, 4:31 p.m. ET

If Adam Rippon and Jenna Johnson were taking a chance with a contemporary dance that started inside a bird cage Monday night, it was a chance that solidified him as the favorite to win the mirror ball trophy next week on Dancing with the Stars.

The openly gay Olympic figure skater, wearing a feathered costume worthy of Johnny Weir, scored three 10s and a 9 to highlight the third week of the special four-week all-athletes season and advance to next Monday's finale. The dance was so good that judge Bruno Tonioli told him and partner Jenna Johnson, “It’s impossible to tell who is the professional.”

That’s a compliment to both, not to mention something generally said of winners over the 25 seasons.

“Perfect!” said Shauna Fuhs, the pom coach at Gaithersburg (Md.) High School who has been scoring DWTS for USA TODAY the last three seasons and for herself the last nine. “Wanted to rewind it as soon as it was over. Cool choreography. Really stayed in sync. They have the best partnership of the season.”

The only one who could technically catch him was surprisingly sent home: close friend and fellow 2018 Olympic figure skater Mirai Nagasu. Despite consistent performances, she never seemed to gain favor with the fans, her Valley girl persona perhaps becoming a bit grating.

Which brings us to Tonya Harding, who has made next week’s three-person finale along with Washington Redskins defensive back Josh Norman.

The way Harding was promoted coming into the season as a figure skating legend, it was clear the producers were riding her story and the controversy off the movie about her life. The attack on Nancy Kerrigan and the subsequent revelation of how much she knew were never really mentioned. It was quite telling after her individual dance Monday that judge Carrie Ann Inaba said to her “We’re all in your corner.”

Harding later said “Thank you, America,” for the support. And she cried, as she has each week.

Asked during the show what it would mean to make the final, she said, “That I had the chance and I made it. Whether I win or not, that doesn’t matter. What matters is that I’m here and having a wonderful time, and I don’t want to leave.”

And so we were not able to say, “Bye, Tonya.”

Also sent home were Olympic luger Chris Mazdzer and Olympic softball pitcher Jennie Finch-Daigle.

The clutch performance of the night was by Norman. He scored 36 — second only to Rippon in the individual performance — doing a contemporary dance with partner Sharna Burgess.

Said Fuhs, “Impressive. Showed off his strength and grace at the same time. Seemed very at ease. Sharna always does the best job of choreographing her partner’s stories, which makes them better able to connect with the routine."

Popular baseball player David Ross, the Season 24 runner-up, came back as a guest judge.

The four-week season, a first for the show, allowed no room for error, which should not have bothered athletes who live under that kind of pressure.

For example, Fuhs said of Nagasu: “Quality performance of what we would normally see later in the longer seasons, like week eight. It’s only their third week doing this.”

Yet, that could not overcome a lack of viewer support, though we never see the viewer votes.

What the truncated season did not allow for was the growth you can see over, say, 10 weeks. Even Inaba told Finch-Daigle she wished there were more weeks in the season for her to improve.

Would a longer season have produced a different final three? Rippon was a lock. Some of us thought Nagasu was. Norman was getting better and better. Maybe the question then would have been: Could America have taken six more weeks of Harding?
